The Arithmetic of It

4 min read ·

Frances had not been inside the kirk since she was fourteen, the year she decided she didn't believe in anything.

She stood at the door now, forty-one years old, wearing the wrong coat for the weather. The stone was the same grey-pink granite she remembered. The door was the same heavy oak, but the latch was new — a black iron thing that didn't belong.

Inside, eighteen people sat in pews meant for two hundred. The village had been losing people for forty years.

She took a seat near the back, which felt right. The minister was young — she hadn't expected that — with the kind of face that hadn't decided what it was yet. He spoke about patience in a way that didn't feel practiced. She appreciated that.

She'd moved back to Ardmore in January, after the divorce was finalized and she'd realized that Edinburgh was not, in fact, her city — it had been Martin's city, and she'd borrowed it for seventeen years, and now it was asking for it back.

The cottage belonged to her father, who had died four years ago and left her everything, the way her mother had, before him. She'd meant to sell it. She'd visited six times to assess what needed fixing, and each time she'd left without fixing anything. In January she'd stopped pretending.

After the service, people stood in the narthex and talked. She didn't know how to join them. She recognized a few faces — the pharmacist's wife, the woman who ran the post office, one of her father's old drinking companions whose name she kept losing. They smiled at her. Nobody came over.

An old man she didn't recognize stood near the door, putting on a waterproof jacket with practiced slowness. He was the kind of old man who'd been born looking old. Thick hands. White eyebrows like he'd walked through cobwebs.

"Angus Moffatt's daughter," he said, not as a question.

"Yes."

"He talked about you."

She waited.

"Not often. But when he did." He pulled up the jacket's zip an inch at a time. "You moved back, then."

"I did."

"Good," he said. Like it was decided.

She didn't know what to say to that, so she said, "Were you a friend of his?"

"More than that. Thirty years on the same boat." He said it the way a sailorman speaks of time — measured in seasons and crossings, not calendar years. "He was a good man to be on the water with."

She knew her father had fished. She hadn't known it was thirty years of it. She thought of all the things she hadn't known about him, all the knowing she'd put off and then run out of time to do.

"He was proud of you," the old man said. "The job. The way you carried yourself."

"We weren't close, at the end."

"No." He didn't soften it. "But that's not what I said."

She walked home along the coast road. The water was the color of pewter. A container ship sat on the horizon, barely moving, and she watched it for a moment.

The culture of a small village is not a thing you can explain to someone from outside it. It's the way news moves without anyone sending it. The way the pharmacist's wife had smiled at her — not warmly, not coldly, but with a particular steadiness, like a door left open. The way the old man had said good, and meant stay.

She'd spent most of her adult life in cities, which had taught her to expect nothing from strangers. That expectation now felt like something she could put down.

The cottage was cold when she got in. She filled the kettle, lit the burner. Through the window she could see the hill where the old grazing land had gone to bracken.

She thought about what the old man had said. Not what I said.

Her father had been proud of her. And she hadn't been there to know it, and now she was here, and he wasn't. The arithmetic of that was not fair, and also not anyone's fault. That was the part she kept returning to, because she didn't know what to do with a grief that had no one to blame.

The kettle began to sound. She poured.

She thought: stay.
