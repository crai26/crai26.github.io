After

4 min read ·

The flat trays were stacked on the dining table, each one divided into compartments, each compartment holding a stone. David had been a geologist for forty years, and he'd catalogued everything with the same neat block lettering: the specimen name, the locality, the date of acquisition. His daughter Mara hadn't seen most of them since she was a child.

She picked up a pale green crystal, slightly waxy, the color of old sea glass. The label read: Morenosite. Nickel sulfate. Bilbao, Spain. 1991.

"That one's from the year you were born," he said from the kitchen doorway. He was wearing the old wool jacket she'd sent him — tweeded and slightly too large across the shoulders now, since he'd lost the weight. He looked smaller than she remembered.

"I didn't know that."

"I was at a conference. Your mother called on the hotel phone." He came to the table and stood beside her, not quite touching. "I couldn't get a flight back until the following morning."

Mara set the crystal down. She'd heard this story — or a version of it — many times. In each telling, the detail shifted slightly: sometimes he'd tried to get an earlier flight, sometimes he'd already been on his way home. She had stopped correcting him years ago.

"The label says 1991," she said.

"Yes."

She picked up the next tray. He moved to the window.

The apartment was warm, overheated in the way of old buildings. Outside, the university's elm trees had started to bud, that tentative green that could still be killed by a late frost. In six weeks he would have the surgery; in twelve, if the margins were clean, he might not need anything further. That was what the oncologist had said. Might not need anything further. A precise construction that meant many things.

She'd brought a notebook to help him catalogue what to keep and what to donate to the department. He'd spent most of his career in orismology's borderlands — the precise naming of minerals, of systems, of things that would not behave if left unnamed. She had always thought his love of labels was a form of control, a way of pinning the world in place. Now she thought it might have been something else: a form of care. To name a thing was to say this exists, this matters, I noticed it.

"The green one," she said. "I want to keep it."

He looked at her. "The morenosite?"

"Yes."

He was quiet for a moment. She watched him weigh something — she could see him doing it, could see the small adjustment in his expression that she'd watched her whole life and never quite learned to read.

"It's not a pretty specimen," he said. "There's a fluorite from Madagascar, blue-purple. Much more striking."

"I know. I want the green one."

He went to the kitchen without answering. She heard him fill the kettle. She heard the gas click and catch.

The morenosite sat in its compartment, pale and unremarkable. She'd chosen it not because she liked it but because it was the year she was born, because he'd been somewhere else when she arrived, because he'd carried this small green stone home from Spain and labeled it and kept it for thirty-five years without telling her. That meant something. She was not sure what to call it.

He came back with two cups and set one in front of her.

"You can have the morenosite," he said.

She wrapped both hands around the cup. The tea was too hot. She held it anyway.

"After," he said. He did not say after what. He didn't need to.

"Yes," she said. "After."

Outside, the elms stood in their uncertain green. Mara looked at the stone in its labeled tray and thought: this is what he could say. He could not say I was afraid, I am afraid, I'm sorry I was in Spain, I am sorry I am anywhere that is not here. But he could say after. He could give her the green stone from the year she was born. He could make tea when the words ran out.

She picked the morenosite up again and held it in her palm. It was lighter than it looked. They stayed like that — her with the stone, him with his cup, the elm trees budding outside in the dangerous April warmth — and did not say anything else for a long time, which was its own kind of saying.
