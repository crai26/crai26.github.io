When Water Becomes
a Mirror

The phenomenon begins approximately forty minutes before true dark. The water's surface tension seems to change — not chemically, but optionally. It stops refracting and starts remembering.

In these undeep pools, the light doesn't penetrate so much as linger at the surface. And it's here, in this liminal depth, that you notice the pulse: the rhythmic contraction of muscle tissue in creatures small enough to overlook.

A myocyte is the fundamental unit of muscle — a single cell capable of contraction. In cardiac tissue, they beat in coordinated waves. In these tide pool creatures, at nightfall, they flash.

Cellular Anatomy — Section 4

The Geometry of
Contraction

Myocytes are striated — striped — at the microscopic level. Bands of actin and myosin proteins interleave like fingers clasped in prayer, ratcheting past each other when a calcium signal fires.

The signal travels at 0.5 metres per second. In the scallop, that wave of calcium propagates across an entire mantle in less time than it takes to blink. The shell snaps. The animal jets backward. It survives.

At nightfall, when the light fails and the temperature drops by two degrees, something changes in the rhythm. The intervals lengthen. The contractions deepen. As if the body is settling in for the dark.
