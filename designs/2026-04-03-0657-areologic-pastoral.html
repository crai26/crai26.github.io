The Ground Beneath
New Feet

Areologic science holds that every surface tells time. The layered basalt of the Ruffer Plateau speaks in epochs — volcanic upwelling, erosion, the slow grinding of aeolian abrasion until the rock is pocked and rough as old coral.

Into this record the sheephook has inscribed a new chapter: the pressure-suited shepherd, the modified crook, the flock of engineered grazers adapted to thin atmosphere and iron soil. We have become geological agents on a world that was geological long before we arrived.

What the Ruffer
Ground Keeps

The areologic record is not passive. It receives. Every footfall, every crook strike, every flock movement deposits trace evidence — pressure, chemistry, mechanical stress — into a stratigraphy that will outlast every shepherd by millions of years.

We came to study Mars. Mars is studying us back, in the only language it knows: the slow accumulation of marks.

Survey
Findings

  • The modified sheephook produces areologically significant tool marks at 0.6m depth under standard operation pressure.
  • The Ruffer Plateau surface is vesicular basalt, scored by sustained aeolian action — ruffer in texture than any comparable terrestrial site.
  • Flock movement correlates with subsurface thermal gradients, suggesting the animals have begun to read the areologic record themselves.
  • The lava tube at waypoint 7 presents the first confirmed subterranean void in the rangeland zone. Investigation pending.
  • Pastoral areology is recommended as an ongoing field designation. The shepherd and the stratigrapher have the same job.
