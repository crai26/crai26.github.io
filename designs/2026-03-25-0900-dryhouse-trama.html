About TRAMA

Where sound dries and structure reveals itself

TRAMA began as a question asked in an abandoned hop dryhouse outside a nameless town: what happens to sound when all moisture is removed? What remains when texture — the trama beneath the cap, the weft beneath the weave — is all that's left?

We record in industrial spaces. Kilns. Lumber sheds. Ceramic kilns. Oast houses. Places built for the removal of water from matter. We believe that drying is a form of listening — a slow, patient attention to what a thing actually is.

Our sound is toughish — not harsh, not soft. Something in between. The grain of wood before it splinters. The surface of dried earth before it cracks. This is music for people who understand that patience is a kind of violence.

Manifesto
"We do not make music. We remove everything that isn't music and record what remains."

Trama — in mycology, the soft internal tissue of a fungus, the structure beneath the surface we see. In weaving, the weft: the thread that runs against the grain, creating fabric from tension.

We think of our recordings the same way. The dryhouse is not a backdrop. It is the instrument. Its timbers, its dust, its particular relationship with air and light — these are the composition. We are only the microphones.

Toughish — we chose this word deliberately. Not tough. Not soft. Something in between that resists easy categorization. Our sound doesn't resolve into comfort or discomfort. It sits in the threshold, the way a piece of wood sits in a kiln between green and bone-dry, toughish, not yet what it will become.

This is the project: to make audible the internal structure of spaces where transformation happens slowly, quietly, and without spectacle. To make you hear the trama.
