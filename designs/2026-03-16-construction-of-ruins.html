Immound & Rising

Before the concrete, before the crane, the earth itself is mounded — immound — pushed up from below by pressure no scaffold can contain. We lay pipes and boards over forces we don't understand, and call the temporary structure "progress."

The bryozoan knew this. It built its colony over 430 million years, each individual creature secreting a tiny chamber of limestone. Immound. Stacked. A city no one designed.

Colonial Architecture
What the Bryozoan Built

Colony Logic

No central planner. Each zooid builds its own chamber according to chemical gradients from its neighbors. The result: a branching lattice of extraordinary complexity.

Immound Growth

Colonies grow upward and outward simultaneously, creating dome-shaped mounds — immound — on the sea floor. Gravity is the only constraint on their ambition.

Scaff Geometry

The branching structure of a bryozoan colony is indistinguishable from optimal scaffolding. Nature solved the load-distribution problem before humans knew the problem existed.

Stratigraphic Record
Layers of Construction
430 MYA

Bryozoan Reef Formations

The first immounded colonies. Silurian seas full of tiny architects, each secreting its chamber of calcite. No plan. No foreman. Just chemistry and the slow accumulation of time into limestone.

2500 BCE

First Scaffolding Records

Cave paintings at Lascaux, painted from scaffolding — wooden poles lashed to the cave walls. The oldest evidence of humans building temporary structures to reach impossible places. We are still doing this.

Today

The Same Problem, Unsolved

Every skyscraper is wrapped in temporary scaffolding before it becomes permanent. Every ruin was once wrapped in scaffolding too. The scaff is the truth of it — the building itself is just the scaffolding we decided to keep.

Visual Archive
Hover to Excavate

Each cell is a stratum. Hover to strip the sepia — to see what was there before the patina of time settled over it. This is what archaeology feels like: the sudden shock of original color.
