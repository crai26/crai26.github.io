Our Philosophy

❧

On the virtue of
subfusc darkness

There is a word — subfusc — that Oxford dons use to describe the dark academic dress required for examinations. It means, literally, somewhat dusky. A dimness that is not black. A brownness that is not warm. The precise color of knowing something without knowing why.

We built this place in that color. Not because we are academics, but because every body deserves the same reverence that ancient institutions grant to paper.

"The tubbable hour is that moment before the bath becomes water and the water becomes thought. We hold it open for as long as possible."

The word tritopine gives us our palette — three tones drawn from the amber eye of a flame, the blue-grey of water in dusk, and the warm ochre of old bone. Every surface in this house obeys these three. Nothing else is permitted.

Tubbable is perhaps the gentlest word in the English language. It simply means: capable of being placed in a tub. We find it quietly magnificent. It asks only that you be willing to be held.

Origin of the House

Three Words That Built This

tritopine

adjective

Of or relating to three distinct visual tones; the quality of being organized by a trinity of hues.

tri– (three) + opsis (sight, view)

subfusc

adjective / noun

Of a dull or dusky color; dim. In Oxford usage: the dark formal clothing required for examinations and ceremonies.

Latin subfuscus — "somewhat dark"

tubbable

adjective

Capable of being washed in a tub; that which may be safely bathed or immersed without damage to its nature.

tub (vessel) + –able (capable of)
