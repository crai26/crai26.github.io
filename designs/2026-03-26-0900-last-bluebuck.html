A Chronology of Vanishing

The bluebuck didn't disappear in a single catastrophe. It was a slow erasure — each decade removing a few more animals until there were none left to find.

c. 1652
First European contact
Dutch settlers establish the Cape Colony. The bluebuck is noted — numerous, grazing the Overberg lowlands.
1719
Scientific description
The species is formally described by naturalists. It is named for its blue-grey coat — a colour unique among African antelopes.
c. 1774
Anders Sparrman's report
Swedish naturalist Sparrman travels the Cape. He notes the bluebuck is already becoming rare — hunted, displaced by sheep farming.
1781
Specimens shipped to Europe
Four skins and skulls are dispatched to museum collections in Leiden, Vienna, Paris, and Stockholm. This is all that will survive.
c. 1800
Last confirmed sighting
The last known individual is shot. No one records it as the last. That is the acidness of extinction — it is only named in retrospect.
Today
A genome, partially recovered
Modern aDNA analysis has partially sequenced the bluebuck genome from those four specimens. We understand it better now than the people who killed it did.

On Acidness — the sharp grief of loss

There is a particular quality to extinction grief: it bites differently than ordinary loss. The bluebuck cannot be found in a memory. There is no one left who saw it move. The four specimens in their museum drawers hold something almost unbearable — actual fur, actual bone, actual colour — and yet the thing itself is permanently, irrevocably absent. That gap between the artifact and the animal is acidness: the corrosive taste of a door that was shut before you arrived.
