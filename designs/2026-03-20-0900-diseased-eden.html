Field Note — Specimen A

Where the cowbind refuses to stop climbing

In the language of weeds, cowbind is a poet. It does not ask permission to grow. It finds the crack, the gap, the overlooked seam — and it enters. Slowly at first. Then everywhere at once.

This garden was designed to be beautiful. It was given good soil and clean water. But something in the seed knew to rot first, to ferment in the dark before it rose. What emerged was not quite what was planted.

Horsing through the undergrowth at midnight

There is something animal about how this place moves. Not the slow patience of moss. Something with hooves. Something that tears through the canopy and doesn't wait to see what it has damaged.

The garden responds to force with growth. Every wound becomes an entry point. The pyorrheic bloom — that sickly excess, that overflow — is not a symptom. It is the method. The garden learned to be diseased because disease is how it spreads.

This is what wildness looks like from the inside.
