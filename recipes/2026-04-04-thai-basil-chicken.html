Thai Basil Chicken (Pad Kra Pao)

Serves 4–6
Time 25 min
Difficulty Easy
thai chicken stir-fry weeknight spicy basil

Pad kra pao is Thailand's true national comfort food — the dish millions of Thais eat for breakfast, lunch, and dinner from street stalls and canteen windows. The name means "stir-fried holy basil," and the holy basil is non-negotiable: its peppery, clove-like punch is what separates this from a generic chicken stir-fry. The technique is fast and ferociously hot — the wok should be screaming when the chicken goes in, which builds the charred, slightly smoky edge that makes this so addictive. Each bowl gets a crispy-edged fried egg on top, which you break into the rice so the yolk pools into the sauce.

Ingredients

  • 700g boneless skinless chicken thighs, roughly chopped into 1–2 cm pieces
  • 6 garlic cloves, roughly chopped
  • 6–8 bird's eye chilies, roughly chopped (deseed half for medium heat)
  • 3 shallots, thinly sliced
  • 2 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 1½ tbsp fish sauce
  • 1 tbsp light soy sauce
  • 1 tsp dark soy sauce
  • 1 tsp caster sugar
  • 3 tbsp neutral oil (vegetable or rice bran), divided
  • 2 large handfuls holy basil leaves (or Thai basil — see tip)
  • 4–6 eggs
  • Steamed jasmine rice, to serve
  • Lime wedges, to serve

Method

  1. Mix the oyster sauce, fish sauce, light soy, dark soy, and sugar in a small bowl. Set aside near the hob — everything moves fast once you start.
  2. Use a pestle and mortar to roughly pound the garlic and chilies together into a coarse, fragrant paste. You want texture, not a purée.
  3. Heat a wok or large heavy frying pan over the highest heat you have for 2 minutes. You want it properly ripping hot — a drop of water should evaporate instantly.
  4. Add 2 tbsp oil. When it just begins to smoke, add the garlic-chili paste and shallots. Stir-fry for 30–45 seconds until fragrant and the garlic starts turning golden at the edges.
  5. Add the chicken in a single layer. Leave it undisturbed for 60 seconds to develop a little char, then stir-fry vigorously for 3–4 minutes until cooked through and slightly caramelised in places.
  6. Pour in the sauce mixture. Toss everything together for 1 minute over high heat until the sauce reduces and glazes the chicken. Remove from heat and fold in the basil leaves — residual heat will just wilt them while keeping their fragrance.
  7. While the chicken rests, fry the eggs in the remaining 1 tbsp oil in a separate pan over high heat. You want crispy, lacy edges and a runny yolk — about 2 minutes basting with the hot oil.
  8. Serve the chicken over jasmine rice with a fried egg on top and a lime wedge alongside. Eat immediately.

Notes

This is a per-person dish at heart — if cooking for 6, consider doing two quick batches rather than crowding the wok, which would steam instead of char the chicken.

On the basil: Holy basil (กะเพรา, krapao) is the real thing — peppery and slightly clove-like. Thai basil (horapha) is sweeter and more anise-forward and still excellent if that's what you can find. Italian basil is the last resort and will taste noticeably different. Asian grocers usually stock at least Thai basil fresh.
