Moroccan Lamb & Chickpea Braise with Preserved Lemon

Serves 4–6
Time 2 hrs 15 min
Difficulty Medium
lamb moroccan one-pot chickpeas braise spring

Spring lamb and preserved lemon is one of the great flavour alliances of Moroccan cooking — the slow braise pulls collagen from the bone, enriching the sauce while the lemon's salted, fermented rind cuts right through any richness. Ras el hanout (the name means "top of the shop") varies wildly by maker, but its warm mix of cumin, coriander, cinnamon, turmeric, and rose petals gives this dish a depth that's impossible to fake with individual spices. It's a one-pot that rewards patience: the longer it sits, the better it tastes.

Ingredients

  • 1.2 kg bone-in lamb shoulder, cut into large chunks (ask your butcher)
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 large onion, roughly chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, sliced
  • 2 tbsp ras el hanout
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 cinnamon stick
  • 400 g tin whole peeled tomatoes
  • 400 ml chicken or lamb stock
  • 2 × 400 g tins chickpeas, drained and rinsed
  • 1 preserved lemon, pith removed, rind finely sliced
  • 80 g green olives, pitted
  • Large handful fresh coriander, roughly chopped
  • Small handful fresh mint leaves, to finish

Method

  1. Pat the lamb dry and season generously with salt and pepper. Heat the olive oil in a large Dutch oven or heavy-based pot over medium-high heat. Brown the lamb in batches — don't crowd the pan — until deeply golden on all sides, about 4 minutes per batch. Transfer to a plate.
  2. Reduce heat to medium. Add the onion to the same pot and cook, stirring occasionally, until softened and lightly golden, 6–8 minutes. Add the garlic and cook for 1 minute more.
  3. Stir in the ras el hanout, cumin, and cinnamon stick. Cook for 30 seconds until fragrant, then add the tinned tomatoes, crushing them roughly with a spoon.
  4. Pour in the stock and return the lamb (and any resting juices) to the pot. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat to low, cover, and braise for 1 hour 30 minutes, turning the lamb once halfway through.
  5. Uncover and stir in the chickpeas, preserved lemon rind, and olives. Continue cooking, uncovered, for 20–25 minutes until the sauce has reduced and the lamb is very tender — it should yield easily when pressed with a spoon.
  6. Discard the cinnamon stick. Taste and adjust seasoning. Stir through most of the coriander.
  7. Serve in wide bowls scattered with the remaining coriander and the fresh mint leaves.

To serve

This is excellent over fluffy couscous (simply cover with boiling salted water, rest 5 minutes, fluff with a fork, finish with butter). Crusty bread or warm flatbread works just as well for mopping up the sauce. A simple cucumber-and-yoghurt salad alongside balances the richness beautifully.

Tip: No preserved lemon? Substitute the zest of 1 lemon added at the end — you'll lose some of the fermented depth, but it still brightens the braise. Ras el hanout varies a lot between brands; if yours is mild, add an extra ½ tsp. If you can't find it, a mix of 1 tsp each of ground coriander and cumin, ½ tsp each of cinnamon and turmeric, and a pinch of cayenne is a reasonable stand-in.
